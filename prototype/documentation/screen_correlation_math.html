Screen-vs-Screen Correlation

RETICLE · mathematics of the similarity metric

Given a query CRISPR screen, RETICLE ranks every other screen by how similar its genome-wide knockout phenotype is. This note defines the exact quantities computed. The design follows a simple observation about screen data: the informative genes sit in the two tails of the ranking, while the large majority in the middle carry little effect and behave like noise. A plain, equal-weight correlation is therefore diluted by that middle mass, so RETICLE uses a weighted correlation that lets the tails dominate.

Scope of the comparison pool. Screens are only ever compared within a homogeneous set: Homo sapiens, assay domain fitness (proliferation / viability), and genome-wide coverage (COVERAGE_TYPE = FULL, i.e. not hit-only). This keeps the comparison apples-to-apples; cross-species, cross-condition and partial-library screens are excluded from this metric.

1. The per-gene score

Each screen \(s\) is represented by its harmonized within-screen rank percentile. For a gene \(i\) measured in screen \(s\),

$$ x_i^{(s)} \;=\; 2\,\frac{\operatorname{rank}_s(i)-1}{N_s-1} \;-\; 1 \;\in\; [-1,\,1], \qquad \text{(within-screen rank percentile)} $$

where \(\operatorname{rank}_s(i)\) is the ascending rank of the gene's harmonized score among the \(N_s\) measured genes of that screen (ties averaged). By construction \(x_i^{(s)}=-1\) is the most depleted / essential end, \(x_i^{(s)}=+1\) the most enriched / knockout-advantageous end, and \(x_i^{(s)}\approx 0\) is the no-effect middle. Using the percentile (not the raw harmonized score) puts every screen on the same bounded, rank-based scale regardless of its assay chemistry or library size.

2. Genes compared: the shared set

Two screens rarely measure exactly the same genes. For a pair \((a,b)\) we only compare over the intersection of measured genes,

$$ \mathcal{G}_{ab} \;=\; \{\, i : x_i^{(a)} \text{ and } x_i^{(b)} \text{ both defined}\,\}, \qquad n_{ab} = |\mathcal{G}_{ab}|. $$

A pair is only reported when the overlap is large enough to be meaningful, \(n_{ab} \ge n_{\min}\) (RETICLE uses \(n_{\min}=200\)). All sums below run over \(i \in \mathcal{G}_{ab}\); we write \(a_i \equiv x_i^{(a)}\) and \(b_i \equiv x_i^{(b)}\).

3. Plain correlation (baseline)

The equal-weight Pearson correlation on the percentiles — because the input is a rank percentile, this is effectively a Spearman correlation on the two screens' gene rankings:

$$ r_{ab}^{\text{plain}} \;=\; \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i} (a_i-\bar a)(b_i-\bar b)} {\sqrt{\displaystyle\sum_i (a_i-\bar a)^2}\,\sqrt{\displaystyle\sum_i (b_i-\bar b)^2}}, \qquad \bar a = \tfrac{1}{n_{ab}}\sum_i a_i,\;\; \bar b = \tfrac{1}{n_{ab}}\sum_i b_i. $$

This treats all \(\sim\!18{,}000\) genes equally. Since most of them sit near \(0\) with near-random ordering, two screens that share real biology on their strongest hits can still score a modest \(r^{\text{plain}}\) — the shared signal is averaged against a large bath of noise.

4. Weighted correlation (the metric RETICLE ranks by)

We give each gene a weight that is large only when the gene is extreme in both screens, so the tail genes drive the statistic and the middle is suppressed:

$$ w_i \;=\; |a_i|\cdot|b_i| \; . $$

(a gene near \(0\) in either screen gets weight \(\approx 0\))

The weighted Pearson correlation then uses weighted means, variances and covariance:

$$ \bar a_w = \frac{\sum_i w_i\,a_i}{\sum_i w_i}, \qquad \bar b_w = \frac{\sum_i w_i\,b_i}{\sum_i w_i}, $$ $$ r_{ab}^{\text{weighted}} \;=\; \frac{\displaystyle\sum_i w_i\,(a_i-\bar a_w)(b_i-\bar b_w)} {\sqrt{\displaystyle\sum_i w_i\,(a_i-\bar a_w)^2}\; \sqrt{\displaystyle\sum_i w_i\,(b_i-\bar b_w)^2}}\;. $$

Both statistics lie in \([-1,1]\). RETICLE ranks candidate screens by \(r^{\text{weighted}}\) and shows \(r^{\text{plain}}\) and \(n_{ab}\) alongside for context.

Why the weight is a product, not a sum. With \(w_i=|a_i|\,|b_i|\) a gene contributes only when it is a strong hit in both screens — exactly the "shared tail" signal. A sum \(|a_i|+|b_i|\) would also up-weight genes extreme in just one screen, which is not evidence of agreement. The product is the natural "both screens care about this gene" weight.

Reading the two numbers together

  • \(r^{\text{weighted}} \gg r^{\text{plain}}\) — the screens agree mainly on their strongest hits (shared core dependencies) while the bulk of the genome is uncorrelated. This is the common, informative case.
  • \(r^{\text{weighted}} \approx r^{\text{plain}}\), both high — broad, genome-wide agreement (e.g. replicate screens of the same project).
  • Both low — little shared signal even among the hits.

The plain correlation, by contrast, gives every one of these genes equal footing — which is why USP5 and the thousands of middle genes drag \(r^{\text{plain}}\) down to 0.43 while \(r^{\text{weighted}}\) reports the true 0.65 agreement on the shared core.

6. Notes and caveats

  • Bounded, robust inputs. Weights use the percentile \(|x_i|\le 1\), so no single outlier gene can dominate through an unbounded raw magnitude.
  • Coverage is surfaced, not hidden. The shared-gene count \(n_{ab}\) is always shown: a correlation over 800 shared genes is not the same evidence as one over 15,000.
  • Replicate screens. Screens from one publication (technical/line replicates) legitimately reach \(r\to 1\); a high score is not by itself independent validation.
  • On-demand, not a stored pair table. For a query screen the metric is computed against all others in the pool at request time (\(\mathcal{O}(P\cdot \bar n)\) for \(P\) pool screens), so no \(\binom{P}{2}\) table is materialized.
  • Hard tail cutoffs vs. weighting. An alternative is to restrict the correlation to the top/bottom \(k\%\) of genes. Weighting is preferred: it needs no arbitrary threshold and transitions smoothly from hit to non-hit.
